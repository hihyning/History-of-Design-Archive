WHAT IS MODERNISM? (1880-1930)

The Modernism design movement, emerging in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, was a radical departure from the past, shaped by the cultural, technological, and societal changes of the time such as industrialization and World War I. Central to its philosophy was the rejection of historical styles and ornamentation, focusing instead on functionality, simplicity, and a minimalistic approach. Modernism emphasized the use of new materials like steel and glass, and championed the idea that form should follow function, prioritizing practicality and efficiency in design. This movement also embraced abstraction and experimentation, seeking to create a universal design language that transcended cultural and national boundaries. It represented a desire for clarity and order in an era of rapid change, and its philosophy of embracing simplicity and functionality in design has continued to influence contemporary design and cultural aesthetics.

BAUHAUS (1919-1933)

Founded in Weimar, Germany in 1919 by Walter Gropius, the Bauhaus was a pioneering school and movement in art, design, and architecture. It operated in Weimar from 1919 to 1925, then in Dessau from 1925 to 1932. The Bauhaus unified art, craft, and technology, emphasizing practical skills and a minimalist aesthetic. Among its alumni, the Bauhaus boasted prolific modernist artists like Wassily Kandinsky and Paul Klee. Its influence on the Modernist design movement was significant, promoting functionalism and the integration of technology in arts. This led to transformative impacts in architecture, graphic design, interior design, and industrial design, characterized by clean lines and geometric forms. The school's legacy continued to shape global art and design throughout the 20th century.

E-1027 (1929)

Architect Eileen Gray in collaboration with Jean Badovici, is a celebrated example of early modernist architecture, located in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin, France. This building is notable for its minimalist design, emphasizing function and simplicity. Gray's innovative approach to the design of E-1027 included the creation of unique furniture pieces and a thoughtful interior layout that harmoniously blended with the natural landscape. The structure's clean lines, open floor plan, and integration of indoor and outdoor spaces significantly influenced the modernist design movement. E-1027 not only challenged the conventional gender roles in architecture and design of its time but also laid foundational principles for modernist aesthetics, focusing on practicality, simplicity, and the seamless fusion of form and function.

GRAND CONFORT (1928)

This iconic chair, by Le Corbusier in collaboration with Pierre Jeanneret and Charlotte Perriand epitomizes the modernist ethos of combining form with function. Characterized by its simple, geometric shapes and emphasis on comfort and practicality, the Grand Confort chair features a minimalist steel frame that showcases a departure from traditional, ornate furniture design. Its use of high-quality leather cushions represents a blend of industrial and luxury materials, aligning with the modernist principle of merging art and industry.

BLACK WHITE YELLOW
(1927)

This is one of Anni Albers' seminal works created during during her time at the Bauhaus. This piece is renowned for its pioneering approach to weaving, where Albers masterfully blends artistic expression with functional design. The work features a striking interplay of geometric patterns and a restrained color palette, primarily black, white, and yellow, showcasing her innovative exploration of color theory and abstract design. This significantly influenced the modernism design movement by elevating textile design to a form of high art. Her approach emphasized the importance of materials and craftsmanship, blending traditional techniques with modernist ideals of simplicity and functionality. At the Bauhaus, Anni Albers was initially directed towards textile work due to gender norms that favored men in disciplines like painting and architecture. However, her innovative work in textiles challenged these norms, elevating weaving to a major art form and paving the way for future female artists in the modernist design movement.

PAVILLION DE L'ESPRIT NOUVEAU (1925)

Designed by Le Corbusier and Pierre Jeanneret and presented at the 1925 International Exhibition of Modern Decorative and Industrial Arts in Paris. This pavilion showcased forward-thinking ideas on urban living and housing. Characterized by its geometric forms, open interior spaces, and the use of industrial materials like steel and glass, the pavilion was a manifesto of Le Corbusier's vision for modern architecture. It proposed a new urbanism with standardized, affordable housing and emphasized functionality and simplicity. The design's modular construction and emphasis on the rational use of space profoundly influenced the development of the International Style in modernist architecture, shaping future urban development.

RED AND BLUE CHAIR (1917)

Designed by Dutch designer Gerrit Rietveld this chair is a pioneering example of modernist design, distinguished by its bold use of primary colors and a striking geometric form. Reflecting the De Stijl movement's principles, it showcases an innovative approach to design through its abstraction, use of straight lines, and planar surfaces. Beyond its aesthetic appeal, the chair's design emphasized functionality and comfort, challenging traditional furniture concepts. Its assembly from standardized, flat wooden planks was revolutionary, prefiguring the later modernist trend towards industrial production and modular design (think IKEA). Its legacy is evident in numerous subsequent designs that echo its emphasis on simplicity, functionalism, and the harmonious balance of form and function.

REVOLVING
ARMCHAIR (1928)

Designed by Charlotte Perriand, this chair integrates a swivel function that enhances its modernist appeal. This feature, emphasizing mobility and adaptability, reflects the modernist focus on practicality and user experience. Made with contemporary materials like chrome-plated steel and leather, its sleek, functional form contrasts with traditional furniture styles.

TEAPOT (1924)

Marianne Brandt, a Bauhaus student and later a teacher, crafted this teapot with a focus on geometric forms and industrial aesthetics by featuring a combination of circles, squares, and triangles, exemplifies the Bauhaus principle of marrying art with industrial functionality. Made from silver and ebony, it reflects the modernist emphasis on using novel materials and embracing simplicity in design. The teapot's absence of unnecessary ornamentation highlight the ethos of form following function.

WASSILY CHAIR (1925)

This iconic piece was created by Marcel Breuer during his tenure at the Bauhaus, reflecting the school's radical approach. It exemplifies the movement's principles through its innovative use of materials and minimalist aesthetic. Breuer's exploration of tubular steel, a material then primarily used in bicycle construction, marked a significant departure from traditional wood furniture. The chair's design, featuring a bent steel frame and canvas or leather slings, showcases the Modernist emphasis on functionality, simplicity, and the honest use of industrial materials.

TABLE LAMP
(1924)

William Wagenfeld, a student at the Bauhaus, created this lamp to embody the school's principles of functionalism, simplicity, and the use of industrial materials. It demonstrated the potential of industrial materials in household objects.

NATURE MORTE (1920)

Le Corbusier's still life painting, os defined by its abstract form and reduced color palette, encapsulates the Modernist emphasis on minimalism and abstraction. Le Corbusier, primarily known for his architectural work, applied similar principles of functionality and simplicity in his painting. This offers an alternative geometric interpretation that aligns with the cross-disciplinary Modernist ideals.

CHAISE LOUNGE (1928)

Charlotte Perriand's streamlined chair, made from materials like steel and leather, showcases the movement's embrace of industrial materials and minimalist aesthetics. The chair's ergonomic design emphasizes functionality and user comfort, such as the tilted back, reflecting Modernism's focus on practical, user-centric design.

SCREEN (1922)

Eileen Gray's screen, designed displays her mastery of Japanese lacquer techniques, a skill that adds depth and texture to this Modernist piece. This piece stands out for its innovative use of materials and its functional, yet artistic design. Crafted from lacquered wood and metal, the screen exhibits a fluidity of form and an elegance that was avant-garde for its time. Gray's design breaks away from traditional, heavy room dividers, offering instead a lightweight, movable piece that blends utility with aesthetic appeal. The use of lacquered wood and metal in the screen exemplifies a blend of traditional craftsmanship and modern design, featuring a fluid form and elegant, lightweight construction. This innovative approach, combining utility with artistic appeal, aligns with the Modernist emphasis on simplicity and functionality.
